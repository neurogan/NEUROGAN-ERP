3. **Addition to blender.** Operator adds each component to Blend-01 → Verifier confirms addition → both initial.
4. **Blending.** Blend 20 min at standard speed. Record start/stop time and blender revolutions.

9. **Bulk sampling for finished-product testing.** Per Sampling Plan Table H.2.
10. **Bottling, labeling, packaging.** Per SOP-PR-013. Line clearance repeated.
11. **Yield reconciliation.** Actual yield / theoretical yield target 98–102%. Investigate anything outside via OOS path (SOP-QC-006).
12. **QC release.** QC/PCQI reviews BPR, in-process data, and finished COA. Signs Release or Reject per SOP-QC-005.

### F.1 "10% plus or minus" misconception — corrected position

| Prior (incorrect) belief | Correct position |
|---|---|
| A dietary supplement may be within ±10% of the label claim for an active ingredient. | **Wrong for under-dose.** Under 21 CFR 101.9(g)(4) and the FDA Nutrition Labeling Compliance Reference Manual, Class I nutrients (added vitamins, minerals, protein, dietary fiber, potassium — and, by extension, added dietary ingredients where a specific claim is declared) must be **at least 100% of label claim**. Overages are permitted. Shortages are not. Tolerances cited for Class II nutrients (naturally occurring) are **≥ 80%** (not ±10%) and do not apply to declared added ingredient amounts. FDA has consistently treated under-dosed declared dietary ingredients as misbranding and adulteration under FDCA §§ 403(a)(1) and 402(g). |

Neurogan hereby acknowledges this in writing. The release spec going forward is **100% – 120% of label claim** (strength), with overage built into the MMR, not the tolerance.

## H. Sampling Plan

Scope: (i) incoming components for identity + purity (in-house HPLC); (ii) incoming components for contaminants (supplier COA verification); (iii) finished dietary supplements for identity, strength, purity, contaminants (third-party 17025 lab). Replaces "one 50-g sample regardless of lot size" and "one bottle regardless of batch size."

Method: **ANSI/ASQ Z1.4-2013, General Inspection Level II, single normal sampling plan** for attribute inspection, augmented by a minimum of **n = √N + 1** (rounded up) for destructive tests where Z1.4 sample size is impractical. Whichever yields the larger n wins. For each lot, a minimum of **3 containers** must be sampled (one at the beginning, one at the middle, one at the end of the lot) regardless of Z1.4 output.

### H.1 Components — Identity & Purity (in-house HPLC)

| Lot size (containers) | Z1.4 Code Letter | Sample size (containers to open) | Sample per container | Composite mass for HPLC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 – 8 | A | 2 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 10 g |
| 9 – 15 | B | 3 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 15 g |
| 16 – 25 | C | 5 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 25 g |
| 26 – 50 | D | 8 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 40 g |
| 51 – 90 | E | 13 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 65 g |
| 91 – 150 | F | 20 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 100 g |
| 151 – 280 | G | 32 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 160 g |
| 281 – 500 | H | 50 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 250 g |
| 501 – 1,200 | J | 80 | ≥ 5 g | ≥ 400 g |

Identity: one composite per lot (the HPLC method already gives high discrimination). Purity: same composite. Uniformity across containers: if supplier is not yet Approved-Skip-Lot-qualified, test **3 individual containers** (beginning/middle/end) separately before compositing.

### H.2 Components — Contaminants (Supplier COA verification)

Heavy metals (USP <232>), microbial (USP <2021>/<2022>), pesticides (USP <561>), residual solvents (USP <467>). Verify supplier COA on every lot for the first 5 lots per supplier. Thereafter, **skip-lot (1 of every 3 lots)** permitted for a supplier with zero adverse findings; reset to every-lot on any excursion.

| Batch size (bottles) | n (bottles tested) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| ≤ 500 | 3 | Z1.4 GLII code G → n=8 via attribute, but destructive testing constraint; minimum 3 across beginning/middle/end of bottling run |
| 501 – 1,500 | 5 | |
| 1,501 – 3,200 | 8 | |
| 3,201 – 10,000 | 13 | |
| 10,001 – 35,000 | 20 | |
| 35,001 – 150,000 | 32 | |

Tests per batch:
- **Identity** (HPLC match to reference): 1 composite per sampling level above.
- **Strength** (HPLC quantitation): composite, plus 3 individual bottles (B/M/E) to confirm uniformity. Spec: 100–120% of label claim.
- **Purity** (HPLC, no unknown peaks > 0.5%): composite.
- **Contaminants** (heavy metals, microbial, pesticides, residual solvents): composite.

Retention samples: 2× the quantity used for full test, held for shelf life + 1 year per §111.83(b).

## L. Current Workflows (As-Is, 2026-04-20)

Documented for the inspector and for internal use. This is the reality before the CAPA plan takes effect — it is the state that produced the 13 observations.

### L.1 Facility and team

Single facility at 8821 Production Ave, San Diego. All manufacturing is in-house — no co-manufacturers, no contract packagers, no bulk-import finishing. Team across production, warehouse, and QC is 6–12 people.

### L.2 Production trigger

Production runs are triggered manually — a co-founder or ops lead decides "make 500 bottles of X" based on gut feel and Shopify sales velocity. There is no reorder-point-driven work order, no MRP calculation, and no formal demand plan.

### L.3 Component receiving and incoming QC

Warehouse receives the delivery. A ~50 g sample is pulled and walked to the chemist. There is no formal receiving log, no quarantine hold, no written visual-inspection checklist. Supplier COA is accepted as presented. Material moves into usable inventory while HPLC is pending. One HPLC on site. One chemist operates it. Raw data and chromatograms live on the chemist's local PC. No central analytical data archive.

### L.4 Recipe, weighing, and blending

"Recipes" are paper printouts on the floor — one page per product. No digital source of truth. Operators weigh against the recipe sheet; weighing is performed by one person, there is no second-person verification, and no timestamped weighing record. Weights are not captured into any system. Blend time, speed, and operator are not logged in any digital form.

### L.5 Encapsulation, bottling, labeling

Pre-printed labels are ordered in bulk per SKU from an external print vendor. The base label carries no lot or expiry. Lot code and expiry are applied at the packaging line via a thermal-transfer printer. Label spools are stored at the line, not in a controlled cage. Issued-versus-used-versus-returned label reconciliation is not performed.

### L.6 Lot code assignment

Lot codes are hand-written or typed at batch start by whoever is running the line. There is no controlled lot-number format and no system-generated lot code. Lot codes are not systematically linked to inbound component lots.

### L.7 Finished-goods QC, release, and inventory

There is no release SOP and no QC signature on finished batches prior to distribution. Finished goods go into Extensiv (the warehouse/3PL platform used as the inventory source of truth for finished product). Inventory for raw materials, work-in-progress, and anything that isn't bottled finished goods is tracked in spreadsheets.

### L.8 Fulfillment and shipping

In-house pick and pack out of Extensiv, with carrier labels generated through Shopify/ShipStation. No lot-level traceability from Shopify order to the specific fulfilled bottle lot.

### L.9 Complaints

All complaints land in Gorgias. CS agents process refunds through Shopify and instruct customers to keep the product or discard it. No physical product is returned to Neurogan. No quality-vs-AE classification macro. No required lot-code field on the ticket. No routing of quality or adverse-event tickets to a qualified QC reviewer.

### L.10 Returns

Because customers do not ship product back, there is effectively no physical returns flow at present. For the rare cases where product does come back (e.g., refused delivery, damaged shipment), there is no quarantine area and no written disposition procedure.

### M.3 The single biggest gap

The ERP has **no authentication layer** wired up today. Every API route accepts `reviewedBy`, `qcReviewedBy`, and `performedBy` as free-text strings from the request body. The schema's comment "verifiedBy MUST differ from performedBy" is enforced only by a string comparison against another user-supplied string. Until a real identity layer (unique user ID, session, role) is in place, the ERP cannot be cited as a compliant 21 CFR Part 11 record, and any QC signature it produces is not legally defensible. This is the first thing to fix and is the gate for every other ERP-based CAPA action.

### M.4 Rollout gate

No ERP module goes live on the floor until the compliance-ready criteria for that module are met (auth + Part 11 controls + validation script + SOP). Paper BPR and paper release signatures remain the legal record during transition. See `erp-gap-analysis-and-roadmap.md` Section 5 for per-module go-live criteria and Section 6 for the 180-day build sequence.
